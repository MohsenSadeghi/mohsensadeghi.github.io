About IBMD

The IBMD (In-Browser Molecular Dynamics) is a web application that performs Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations and shows the results in real-time (in the computational sense of the word), all inside a standard web browser. The details of this particular simulation case are given in the following sections.

The IBMD lets you see physical and thermodynamic properties of the simulated system as the simulation progresses. The web application relies on Javascript for doing the necessary computations and employs WebGL and HTML5 capabilities for presentation. The processes are all client-side and they use your computer's, tablet's, or phone's CPU. The simulation has no server-side computational cost. So, feel free to continue the simulation for as long as you like!

Case 9: Water-Graphene.3D.AMBER-SPC/Fw-LJ.NVT


Simulation box

The simulation box consists of 25 water molecule on a graphene sheet. Periodic boundary conditions are applied in the X, Y, and Z directions. As the graphene sheet extends in the X and Y directions to limits of the simulation box, the application of priodic boundary conditions makes it an infinite sheet.

The minimum image convention is used to handle the periodic images in calculating interaction energies and forces.

Statistical ensemble

The simulation is carried out in the canonical or NVT ensemble, which means that the number of particles (N), the volume of the system (V), and its temperature (T) are constant.

Interactions

Interactions in this system consist of covalent bonds between carbon atoms that form the graphene sheet, intramolecular forces of water molecules and non-bonded water-water and water-graphene interactions. Carbon sp2 covalent bonds are modeled using harmonic potentials due to bond-stretching and angle-bending. The parameters of these harmonic potentials are taken from the AMBER forcefield. For water molecules, the Simple Point Charge/Flexible or SPC/Fw model is used. The parameters of the SPC/Fw potential are taken from this SklogWiki page. Non-bonded interactions are modeled using the Lennard-Jones 6-12 potential. The parameters of the Lennard-Jones potential for carbon atoms are taken from the AMBER force field.

Lorentz-Berthelot mixing rule is used for crossed interactions. You may visit this SklogWiki page on different mixing rules.

No cut-off other than what is forced by the minimum image convention is used.

Time integration

Equations of motion are integrated using the velocity Verlet algorithm. You can learn more about this algorithm from this Wikipedia page on Verlet integration.

Due to the presence of strong electric interactions, a small timestep of 2 fs is used for time integration to handle rapid molecular vibrations.

Thermostat

In order to maintain constant temperature in the simulation, the Berendsen thermostat is used. The Berendsen thermostat acts through scaling velocity vectors based on the ratio of current temperature to the setpoint temperature.

Output

To the right of the page, you see an animated 3D output showing the positions of particles in the simulation box. All particles are illustrated by spheres with radii equal to the radii of atoms in covalently-bonded molecules.

To the left, there is a table displaying thermodynamic properties of the system. Using Real-time Plot option, you can plot graphs of these properties versus time and see them fluctuate as the simulation progresses or as you use the controls.

Controls

The simulation starts by pressing the Start button and stops by pressing Stop. You can resume a stopped simulation by pressing the Start button agian. You can also restart a simulation, whether running or stopped, to the initial state by pressing the Reset button.

You can change setpoint temperature of the thermostat using T ▲ and T ▼ buttons. When the setpoint temperure changes, the Berendsen thermostat starts adjusting the system temperature to match the setpoint value. But this process takes time and you have to wait briefly for the system temperature to reach the designated value. You can check this by plotting temperature versus time and monitoring the fluctuations.

To the left of the visual output frame, you see camera controls, up, down, pan, tilt, etc. Using these buttons, you can move your viewpoint inside the scene and change the viewing angle to better see the details of the 3D simulation. Apart from these buttons, you can drag on the visual frame to tilt/pan the camera and use mouse wheel for forward/backward movements. There are also keyboard shortcuts associated with most of camera movements. Hover over each camera control buttons to see the respective keyboard shortcut.
